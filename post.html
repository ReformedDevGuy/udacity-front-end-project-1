Range Report: .224 Valkyrie Pt. 2

Testing Our Accuracy Node

John Larson is a Software Engineer at Miller Technology Partners. When he is not at the range (or at his reloading bench), John enjoys spending time honing his skills in React and developing web apps. USA born and bred, John has been involved in competition shooting since he was 16 competes in PRS-Class Long Range Competitions where he has captured an outstanding 3 gold medals, 12 silver medals and 27 bronze medals over his career.

Last week, we tested our ladders with both CFE223 and Varget. While CFE223 provided some stunning velocity numbers (about 50fps more than Varget), our accuracy node was quite low - lower than I want out of a cartridge like .224 Valkyrie - remember, we are going for a 1500 yard shot!

Thankfully, Varget came through yet again and gave us a pretty impressive .6 grain cushion right at our 2750 mark we were trying to hit. Today, we are going to explore that accuracy node and find our best grouping. I'll be shooting my same gun out of a vice to take the "human factor" out of the equation, and we will shoot 5 rounds of each load, with a 1 minute cooldown between shots, and a 15 minute cooldown between loads. We will follow that up with 5 more rounds for each load, in reverse order, expanding our cooldown times to 5 mintues between shots and 1 hour between loads.

As you can see from our results, we tested a mini-ladder from 27.4 grains all the way to 28 grains. Each load was hand measured in 0.1 grain incriments, measured with 0.001 grain accuracy. We managed to put all shots within half MOA at 100 yards, with our best load of 27.7 grains shooting an outstanding 0.26 MOA!

The .224 Valkyrie will never become an accurate cartridge at 1500 yards. Mark my words.

After hearing several horror stories from fellow shooters about how difficult it is to find an accurate load for the .224 Valkyrie, I have to say I am astounded by my results from this experiment. Of course, we still have to load up some bulk loads with our newly-decided charge weight, but as we all know, Varget tends to meter consistently, so I have no doubts we will be ringing steel at 1500 with ease.
